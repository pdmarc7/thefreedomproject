Can Sustainable Tourism Stop A Thawing Glacier In Switzerland?

06 July, 2024

In picturesque Grindelwald, Switzerland, almost every discussion about sustainable tourism starts and ends with a glacier.

The Great Aletsch Glacier is the largest in the Alps, spread across 31 square miles of rugged granite mountains. And it's melting.

In the terminal of the new Eiger Express, a gondola that whisks you to the historic cog railroad, there's an image of the Aletsch in 1850 and another one of the present day. In the 19th century, the glacier covered most of the valley; now it has retreated to the top of the mountain.

The black-and-white images are a stark reminder of how much Grindelwald has already lost — and of the urgency of becoming sustainable.

"Sustainability is part of what we do — and actually, it has always been a part of what we've done," says Kathrin Naegeli, a spokeswoman for Jungfraubahnen, which runs the local trains and cableways.

Back in 1908, four years before completing the final phase of the railroad to the top of the iconic Jungfraujoch, the company built a hydroelectric plant in nearby Lütschental to supply green energy to the new train. Today, the newly refurbished plant with two large turbines powers trains, cable cars and ski lifts in the Jungfrau Region including the ones in and around Grindelwald.

Grindelwald aspires to become one of the most sustainable destinations in Switzerland. Some of its hotels are going even further with new programs that are meant to keep guests thinking about recycling and renewable energy. The village is also doing its best to keep sustainability on the front burner as it tries to preserve its cultural heritage.

How is Grindelwald pursuing sustainable tourism?

When you live under a melting glacier, sustainability becomes imperative. And that's what's happening in Grindelwald.

Mass transit

Grindelwald strongly encourages guests to travel there by train. When you check into a hotel in Grindelwald, your receptionist will hand you bus passes that allow you to get around town. Visitors can also purchase a Jungfrau Travel Pass, which offers unlimited travel on all Jungfrau Railway experience mountains.

Recycling

Grindelwald, like other Swiss cities, has strict waste management policies. That includes recycling programs and efforts to reduce single-use plastics. Hotels encourage their guests to participate in these initiatives.

Controlled development

The village carefully regulates new construction to preserve its Alpine character and prevent overdevelopment. There's a restriction on owning second homes, and new projects like the planned refurbishment of the Grindelwald-First gondola, scheduled for 2030, must undergo rigorous public vetting. Even sustainable projects like a planned solar farm are not automatically approved.

But spend a little time in Grindelwald, and you'll discover the commitment to sustainability runs far deeper.

Going above and beyond at Bergwelt Grindelwald

New hotel projects often try to exceed the standard for sustainability. For example, the Bergwelt Grindelwald, a new luxury hotel, was built to the highest standards of sustainability, says general manager Tanja Münker. But that hasn't stopped it from reaching even higher. It just obtained an elusive IBEX certification, which she describes as a rigorous, 360-degree review of its sustainability practices.

"As a result of the review, we're starting up a program to reduce food waste in September," says Münker.

There are other benefits, too. As only the second hotel in Grindelwald to be IBEX certified, it will have an advantage with some meetings and incentive groups. Meeting planners often give preference to hotels with well-developed sustainability plans.

She says part of the hotel's mission is to remind guests of the importance of sustainability, beyond free bus passes and the obligatory notices in the bathrooms to reuse your towels. Münker says the spectacular alpine scenery takes care of the rest. When people see the beauty of the mountains, they understand how fragile it is. They get it.

On the ascent to First Mountain, a reminder of what's been lost

Just around the corner from Bergwelt Grindelwald, there's a cable car that takes you to the summit of First Mountain. It's the side of the mountain range that gets all the sun during the early part of the ski season, and as you ascend you can see the famous Eiger, the Schreckhorn, the Wetterhorn and the Fiescherlücke with the lower Grindelwald Glacier.

Sandra Kaiser, a local guide, surveys each glacier carefully.

"My parents can remember a time when the glaciers were all the way down to there," she says, pointing about halfway to the mountain. "Now they are up there," she adds, looking to the top of the mountain, where the glaciers now end abruptly.

Climate change is the last thing you want to think about when you're on First Mountain. There are mountain bike rentals and ziplining and a spectacular hike to the Lake Bachalp.

But once you've seen the "before" and "after" images of Aletsch in the terminal, you can't help but mourn how much we've lost because of climate change, and how long it will be before all the glaciers are gone.

There are efforts to reverse — or at least slow — the process. But even in Switzerland, resources are limited.

Building a new thermal energy plant

Grindelwald is doing the best it can, say tourism officials. Melanie Trummer, a product manager at Grindelwald Tourism, says the community's most notable sustainability project is a wood-fired thermal power station. It uses a process that converts the wood into lean gas, bringing it significantly closer to true carbon-neutral energy. It's the first thermal power station in Switzerland that uses a full de-steaming process, including heat recovery and combustion air conditioning.

The combustion of wood chips, sawmill waste, and other waste wood from the region generates carbon-neutral heat energy. From there, long-distance lines connect businesses and households to the power station.

"This can cover 100% of their energy requirements for heating and hot water," she says.

That, combined with the new solar farm planned by Jungfrau Railways and a more energy-efficient cable car to First Mountain that will be built in five years, represents an outsize effort for a small destination like Grindelwald.

But is it enough?

Can Grindelwald save its glaciers?

The glaciers of Grindelwald are incomparably beautiful. Even on a recent snowy day, visitors from the top of the Jungfraujoch, the highest railway station in Europe, seemed to be mesmerized by the solid ice running through the alpine gorge. No wonder it's a UNESCO world heritage site, and people want to preserve it.

But will hydroelectric power, a food waste program or a solar farm really make a difference? When it comes to tourism and sustainability, that is the enduring question. Why bother when just downwind there are coal-powered plants and where sustainability is just a marketing gimmick?

For the Swiss, and particularly for Grindelwalders, watching their beloved glaciers recede a little bit every year has spurred them into action. They are doing everything in their power to preserve their heritage, even though they know it will not be enough.

For visitors, there is a different sustainability imperative. They are coming to Grindelwald by the busload to see the glacier while it's still here.
