Disney VR Investment May Yield ESPN Playerâ€™s-Eye-View Headsets

23 July, 2024

As ESPN charts its direct-to-consumer future with the coming launch of Venu this fall and the company’s own streaming service next year, executives are also experimenting with how the sports conversations of tomorrow will be viewed. 

Last year, Disney CEO Bob Iger teased the ability to watch five ESPN programs at once inside Apple’s Vision Pro device. Bristol has also added a Mandalorian-inspired augmented reality studio capable of dropping hosts into any virtual location. An ESPN-branded experience in Meta Horizon Worlds is due this summer, offering VR highlights.

And behind the scenes, ESPN production leaders have been working with sports tech and gaming company StatusPro. While the collaboration hasn’t gotten the full greenlight yet, the goal is to allow fans to view action literally from a player’s perspective, as talent breaks down the athlete’s thought process.

“We could effectively have talent put a headset on, and then the talent is seeing what Dak Prescott is seeing on the field,” ESPN VP of sports business development and innovation Kevin Lopes said. “It’s just an elevation of telestration.”

The tests follow StatusPro’s participation as one of five members of the 2024 Disney Accelerator class, alongside three AI firms and an autonomous vehicle maker. Disney—which typically identifies potential partners among growth stage companies for its program, unlike other accelerators’ focus on nascent startups—invested in StatusPro as part of the deal.

“It almost does it an injustice to call it an accelerator program,” StatusPro co-founder and president Andrew Hawkins said. “They essentially bring you into the Disney family, and from day one you feel like a Disney company.”

Departments beyond ESPN could end up using StatusPro tech as well. As part of the program, StatusPro bosses met with executives from all of Disney’s business units, and they discussed several potential collaborations at the accelerator’s concluding demo day.

“There are multiple opportunities,” Disney Accelerator GM Bonnie Rosen said. “ESPN is going to be the first point of contact for them. We intend and hope that that will grow.”

Investment details have not been made public, but Disney has retained the opportunity to put more capital into its new partner. 

StatusPro announced a $20 million Series A funding round in February, with big-name backers including Google Ventures, Verizon Ventures, LeBron James, Lamar Jackson and Naomi Osaka. In 2022, the company launched the first NFL and NFLPA-licensed VR simulation game, NFL Pro Era, on Meta’s Quest platform amid a resurgence in excitement around face-based wearable devices. 

In the game, players compete from the perspective of the QB, making play calls, reading coverages and completing throws with hand-tracking controllers. StatusPro says more than a million people have played Pro Era, which it claimed is the fastest selling VR sports title on the Meta platform.

But, Hawkins said, the duo has had its eyes on potential offerings beyond video games since the company first started. 

“We’re finally executing on some ideas we discussed deeply seven years ago,” he said. “So you can imagine what we have in the hopper.”

In a best-case scenario, the new technology could improve on-screen dialogue, creating more opportunities for detailed breakdowns rather than uninformed takery. For now, StatusPro relies on simulated plays, though tracking technology is advancing at such a pace that the possibility of recreating NFL action in the virtual plane is no longer far-fetched. Such tools were used for ESPN’s Toy Story-themed football broadcast last year, for instance. 

“From video you kind of see it from the bird’s eye view where you can see everything—that’s why it’s easy to criticize, right?” StatusPro co-founder and CEO Troy Jones said. “But when you actually get down and you see all the things that are happening, the speed at which it’s moving, that’s what gives you a little different lens and hopefully a little bit more empathy.”

ESPN crews have already tested VR headsets as live production equipment, using Meta gear to help embed its commentators inside the animated world of an augmented NHL broadcast in March. 

The Disney partnership also comes as the entertainment behemoth expands its gaming ambitions. Disney invested $1.5 billion in Epic Games (another accelerator alumnus) in February with plans to create new games and an “entertainment universe.” Iger called the project “probably our biggest foray into the game space ever.”

“When I saw the amount of time [young people] were spending in terms of total media screen time on video games, it was stunning to me,” Iger said at the time.

In response, the company is clearly looking to build appealing games of its own. But on the TV side of things, executives are also attempting to integrate the language of video games into coverage of IRL performances (even if that doesn’t mean explicitly airing esports). 

Disney is also hopeful that StatusPro will expand its connections to other broadcasters. “There’s that component of the venture piece where we want them to succeed as a company,” Disney VP of corporate innovation David Min said.

Shows are generally headed in one of two directions: OK being intermittently tuned out as comforting, background noise; or demanding attention by offering an interactive, immersive, fast-paced experience.

That’s where companies with gaming expertise such as StatusPro come in. After all—interactive, immersive, fast-paced—doesn’t that sound a lot like playing quarterback?
