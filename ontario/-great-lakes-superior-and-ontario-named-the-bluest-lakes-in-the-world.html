We have already established that Lake Superior is the deepest and the largest of the Great Lakes. But where does it rank among the bluest?

There’s nothing quite like the natural beauty of a shimmering blue lake, and there are more than 304 million lakes on Earth, according to Britannica, making up around 2.8% of the planet's land surface area, according to LakeScientist.com.

A brilliant blue color was discovered accidentally by Oregon State University chemists in 2009 while researching materials for electronics applications. Known as known as YInMn Blue or "MasBlue," the pigment contains the elements yttrium, indium, manganese and oxygen, and has become the gold standard and truest shade of blue. Crayon even added it to its collection.

CanadaCasino did a color analysis of popular lakes worldwide and compared it to YInMn Blue.

Using unedited satellite images of more than 80 lakes worldwide, a color match tool was used to find the average HEX code of each body of water. Each shade was then compared to YinMn Blue. The color difference was measured using a DeltaE scale, where the smaller the number, the closer the lake’s color is to YinMn Blue.

Here's what the analysis found.

  • Two Great Lakes — Superior and Ontario — made the Top 10 list.
  • Lake Ontario is considered the bluest lake in the world, with a difference of only 5.32 from YinMn Blue.
  • Lake Superior ranks as the second bluest lake in the world, with a difference of 7.63 from the truest shade of blue.

Lake Baikal in Russia, the No. 7 bluest lake in the world, also ranks No. 7 on the largest lakes in the world, at 12,200 square miles.
