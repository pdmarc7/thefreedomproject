Top five stories you may have missed this week

06 July, 2024

US warns of China meddling after EU sanctions Luxembourg-linked space firm

After the EU slapped sanctions on a raft of Chinese companies - including a firm with a former subsidiary in Luxembourg - the US is warning of mounting evidence that China is fuelling Russia’s war in Ukraine.

The European Union as part of its 14th sanctions package last week adopted restrictive measures against 19 Chinese companies it believes support Russia’s war in Ukraine. That list includes Changsha Tianyi Space Science and Technology Research, which operated a subsidiary in Luxembourg under the name Spacety.

The US Treasury already in January last year had sanctioned Spacety for supplying satellite images of Ukraine to Russian company Terra Tech, which in turn has ties to the Wagner mercenary group.

“It was, for us, a very pleasant turn of events,” US ambassador to Luxembourg Thomas Barrett said about the EU sanctions in an interview. “The European Union recognised that right here in Luxembourg, you had a Chinese company that was supplying visual assistance to Russia.”

Banks push for lighter regulatory burden in wake of ING chaos

ING’s decision to abruptly end its mass retail banking business in Luxembourg has raised complaints over regulatory burdens from the banking sector that says it is faced with ever-mounting obligations.

Requirements such as know your customer (KYC) procedures for clients mean it can take weeks to open a bank account, requiring a proof of address, ID card, tax number and certificate of residence.

For banks, they require resources and create costs that put “their profitability to the test” and “may lead certain players to question the continuation of certain service offerings,” banking sector association ABBL said in June after ING announced it would kick out up to half of its 90,000 clients in Luxembourg.

Minister orders audit of refugee office after Pirate Party controversy

Family Minister Max Hahn has ordered an audit into the National Reception Office after the Luxembourg Times reported exclusively on a dispute about taxpayer money between the government office and the Pirate Party whom it paid to develop a translation app.

Last month, this newspaper together with the Luxemburger Wort reported that the National Reception Office (Office National de l’Accueil - ONA), which is responsible for housing asylum seekers, has been wrestling with the country’s Pirate Party for over nine months to reclaim €92,000.

EU prosecutor to get less than half of extra funding sought for 2024

The EU’s Luxembourg-based fraud-fighting body is set to receive just under half of the extra funding it has requested for this year, as it prepares for Sweden to become the latest country to join.

The European Public Prosecutor’s Office (Eppo) made an urgent request in February for an additional €7.8 million in funding this year, a nearly 10% rise on its current budget, along with more than 150 office staff and 26 EU delegated prosecutors.

Luxair launches Rotterdam route amid calls for fewer short-haul flights

Luxembourg airline Luxair on Wednesday announced it would add the Dutch city Rotterdam to its destinations - only a day after Greenpeace called for fewer short-haul flights to and from the Grand Duchy.

From 27 October, passengers will be able to book a flight between Rotterdam and Luxembourg, Luxair said in a press release. Covering the distance will only take one hour, and return tickets will start at €99, the Luxemburger Wort reported.

The company said the new connection would ease access for people travelling to The Hague and students attending the University of Delft.
