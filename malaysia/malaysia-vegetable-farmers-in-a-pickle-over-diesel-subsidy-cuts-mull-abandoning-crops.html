Malaysia vegetable farmers in a pickle over diesel subsidy cuts, mull abandoning crops

23 July, 2024

“Our farms use a lot of machinery to do things like irrigation to earthworks, and this uses a lot of fuel,” Lim Ser Kwee, the association’s president, told This Week in Asia.

“The prices of pesticides and fertiliser have gone up, vegetable prices are volatile and our members are also struggling to find farming land as many owners no longer want to rent out their land for agriculture. It is becoming more difficult for us.”

The government had promised to keep a lid on inflation after diesel prices surged to 3.35 ringgit per litre (71 US cents) overnight on June 10, when blanket subsidies were replaced with a targeted system aimed at curbing rampant smuggling and saving the government about 4 billion ringgit annually.

On Monday, Anwar said authorities would temporarily shelve plans to extend the subsidy scheme’s rationalisation to the widely used RON95 grade petrol.

“The government has a targeted mechanism that includes the farmers. The problem is in the implementation,” said James Chin, a Malaysia specialist with the University of Tasmania’s Asia Institute.

Malaysia had a long-running issue with poor implementation of government policies, which critics claimed was due to the incompetence of government agencies or alleged sabotage by some civil servants, Chin said.

“I don’t know who to believe, but all we know is that the situation is creating a lot of anger … because people who are supposed to be cost-neutral by getting the subsidies are not getting the subsidies.”

Bank Muamalat’s chief economist Mohd Afzanizam Abdul Rashid agreed that efficient implementation of the targeted subsidy programme was key to addressing public complaints including in the agriculture sector, which employs nearly 1.9 million people, or more than 10 per cent of the country’s total workforce of about 17 million.

“Maybe there could be gaps in terms of the timeliness for the eligible recipients to receive the subsidies, which can affect their cash flow,” Afzanizam said.

Failure to effectively roll out targeted subsidies for diesel and eventually petrol could spell economic disaster given the country’s huge car population, analysts say. According to government data, there were over 36 million vehicles registered in Malaysia as of October last year, higher than the country’s population of 34 million.

Petroleum products carried significant weight among the items comprising Malaysia’s consumer price index – a leading indicator of inflation – at 5.5 per cent, compared with 0.2 per cent for diesel, Afzanizam said.

Lim said the targeted diesel subsidy system has left out many members of the vegetable farmers association based on the revenue range to qualify for government aid, which was set at between 50,000 ringgit and 300,000 ringgit per annum.

Local vegetable producers also faced stiff competition from much cheaper vegetables imported from countries like China, Thailand and Vietnam, Lim said. They also had to deal with wholesalers who set low purchase prices that did not account for rising production costs, he added.

“We’re not asking for diesel subsidies because we know that will be difficult. What we are asking for is some sort of incentive for production, maybe at 120 ringgit per tonne, so that we can match the low prices offered in the market,” Lim said.

“This way ... vegetable farmers profit and consumers also benefit from low prices.”
