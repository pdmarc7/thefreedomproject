Born from humble beginnings in York County, Ann B. Barshinger lived a long life – leaving a lasting impact on more than a million people in York and her later home of Lancaster County.

The philanthropist died Sunday at age 100, but her donations will keep her memory alive.

Barshinger was considered the matriarch of Lancaster General Health's Ann B. Barshinger Cancer Institute.

"She believed in this cancer institute. She would tell whoever she could about the investment she made, why she made it, why it was important," said Jennifer Groff with the Lancaster General Health Foundation.

Barshinger made the initial leadership donation to build the center that bears her name and got more than 800 others to follow. She visited often.

"We found every opportunity to bring Ann in to interact with our staff and our patients, and our staff and patients loved seeing her," Groff said.

Groff said a million patients have come through the door since the cancer center opened.

"All of those lives have been touched by Ann Barshinger's generosity," she said.

Barshinger also had an interest in a variety of organizations and nonprofits.

That included Aaron's Acres, which provides a summer camp experience for children with disabilities.

"She saw the impact. She saw the smiles. And she said, 'It's all about the kids,'" Executive Director Risa Paskoff said.

Paskoff and Barshinger shared a special bond. Paskoff will be forever grateful for the friendship and Barshinger's stewardship of Aaron's Acres.

"In large part, we are where we are because Ann believed in us and always gave, and so everybody at Aaron's Acres is grateful and should be grateful for her belief in our organization, for supporting us over the years," she said.
